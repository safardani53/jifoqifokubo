Aberdeen's new stadium plans could be wrecked by remains of PLAGUE victims - as expert warns disease

A FOOTY club’s plans for a new stadium could be derailed by plague victims, it’s been claimed.

The Dons have spent years looking for a new home and reckon they’ve found the ideal spot close to Aberdeen beach.

But former councillor Muriel Jaffrey thinks there could be massive problems in store because they’ll be building on the graves of black death victims.

The granny, who was on the planning committee for almost two decades, has been told the disease is still active for hundreds of years and is concerned about what could be dredged up.

Muriel, 82, said: “After everything we’ve been through with Covid this is the last thing we need.

“The bodies, or what’s left of them, of hundreds of plague victims are down there.

“The disease could still be active. They shouldn’t be building anywhere near the remains. This could turn into a nightmare for everyone.”

Aberdeen was rocked by the plague at least three times but it’s thought the worst incident came in 1647.

Historians reckon about 1,700 people died and victims were stuck in huts close to the beach.

Those that died were burned and buried in mass graves in the area.

This is where Aberdeen FC want to create a replacement for ageing Pittodrie.

It will be part of a huge redevelopment of the area and plans are well underway.

Former Nats councillor Muriel revealed that in the past a planning application was rejected because it would have involved disturbing the plague pits.

Muriel revealed: “I spoke to someone at Edinburgh council and they told me they will never build anything on their plague graves.

“I do not understand why I seem to be the only person in the city who is worried about this.

“As far as I can see it’s not been raised as an issue or spoken about. That makes no sense to me."

She’s calling on officials to fully investigate before anymore work is done on the plans.

Muriel added: “I was taught about the plague pits when I was at school. It’s an important part of local history.

"Maybe the people who are at the council now don’t know about it?”
